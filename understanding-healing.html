Understanding Healing

Ligaments are like thick rubber bands holding your bones together. When they are injured, they need time to lay down new tissue and strengthen that tissue so it functions like the original.

The Healing Timeline

Healing is not an overnight process, and because the deltoid ligament is very thick, it can sometimes take a bit longer to heal than typical outside ankle sprains. Generally:

  • Days 1-7 (Inflammation Phase): Focus is on protecting the joint and managing pain/swelling.
  • Weeks 2-6 (Repair Phase): The body lays down new scar tissue to bridge the tear. This tissue is fragile at first.
  • Weeks 6 to Months (Remodeling Phase): The new tissue becomes stronger and aligns itself based on the stresses you put on it (which is why rehab exercises are so important!).
Is pain always bad? No.
It is completely normal to feel mild discomfort or a stretching sensation during rehabilitation. This is just your body stretching and organizing the new healing tissue. Healing is not a straight line, and you may have days where the ankle feels achy.

Why do ligaments take longer to heal than muscles? Muscles have an excellent blood supply, which brings oxygen and nutrients quickly to the site of an injury.

Ligaments, however, have a relatively poor blood supply. Therefore, the biological process of cleaning up damaged tissue and laying down new collagen fibers simply takes more time. Patience and consistent, progressive loading (exercise) are the key drivers of successful ligament healing.
