How This Injury Happens

Most ankle sprains happen when the foot rolls inward. However, a deltoid ligament injury occurs from the opposite motion: when the ankle is forced to roll outward or twist while carrying your body weight.

Common Situations

  • Sports: Sudden changes of direction, pivoting rapidly, or being tackled while the foot is planted on the ground (common in soccer, netball, or tennis).
  • Awkward Landings: Jumping and landing on an uneven surface, or stepping on someone else's foot, forcing your ankle to suddenly collapse inward while the foot turns outward.
  • Slips and Falls: Slipping on wet ground or stairs where the leg slides outward violently.
Remember: Because the deltoid ligament is very thick, it takes a significant amount of force or a very awkward angle to sprain it. It is not your fault—it was simply an unfortunate combination of movement and pressure.

In medical terms, this is called an eversion ankle sprain. Eversion means the sole of the foot turns outward away from the body.

When this happens, the bones on the outside of the ankle (the fibula) compress together, while the structures on the inside (the deltoid ligament) are stretched beyond their normal limits. If the stretching force exceeds the strength of the ligament fibers, they begin to tear. A mild sprain means microscopic tearing, while a severe sprain can involve a complete rupture.
