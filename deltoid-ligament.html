What is the Deltoid Ligament?

Where is it located?

The deltoid ligament is a strong, broad band of tissue located on the inside of your ankle. If you feel the bony bump on the inner side of your ankle (called the medial malleolus), the ligament attaches just below it, spreading out like a fan to connect to the bones of your foot.

Simply put: It's the inner ankle's main "seatbelt," holding the bones together.

What does it do?

Its main job is to provide stability. Specifically, it stops your ankle from rolling too far outward and prevents your foot from flattening excessively. Whenever you walk, run, or change direction, this ligament works hard to keep your ankle joint aligned and secure.

Because it is so thick and strong, injuries here are less common than typical "rolled ankles" (which usually happen on the outside). Consequently, when the deltoid ligament is injured, it often requires a bit more care and time to heal fully.

The deltoid ligament is actually made up of two main layers: a superficial (closer to the surface) layer and a deep layer.

  • Superficial layer: Connects the shin bone (tibia) to the navicular and calcaneus (heel bone). It resists the ankle rolling outward (eversion).
  • Deep layer: Connects the shin bone to the talus (the bone the leg rests on). It is incredibly strong and prevents the talus bone from slipping outward or rotating away from the leg.

Injury typically involves the superficial layer first. Damage to the deep layer usually only occurs in more severe traumas, often accompanied by fractures.
